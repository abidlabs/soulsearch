## Distraction, attention, and the heart

### Distraction, *lahw*, and attention as devotion — [0:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jSxFQSvFdwU&t=0)

Now the word in Arabic for distraction is *ilhaa'*, which means to pull somebody into *lahw*—to bring them into entertainment—which in the dictionary is the "pleasurable occupation of the mind." So who is occupying your mind now? What you give your attention to will determine your reality.

What does that mean? It means that if you are always watching news about crime, you are going to think that crime is far more prevalent than it actually is, and you will be scared. You will be walking around thinking, like Chicken Little, that the sky is falling. So what you give your attention to is going to determine your reality. And what God is asking us is to give our attention to God.

Now what is the word in Arabic for attention? What is attention? In English it has a lot of really interesting meanings. One of them is courtesy—like when you say he was very attentive to me. Attention is what a lover gives their beloved. Attention is also a word for devotion. So your attention is your devotion. It is what you are attending to. It is what you are giving your time to. So in Arabic, one of the words for it is what you are concerned with—it is your *hamm*; it is what preoccupies your mind. It is what your mind is full of.

And so the Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) said, "Whoever makes his concerns—in other words, gives his attention to one thing—Allah will take care of all of his other concerns." We are living in a time where people's concerns are completely fragmented, and so there is all this mental derangement because people are so distracted.

Imam Ali (may God be pleased with him) said—and it is sometimes attributed to the Prophet (peace and blessings upon him)—that people are asleep, and when they die, they awaken. The one who is discerning (*dhaaki*) is intelligent because he is focused: he gives his attention to what is important. The Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) said, in one wording, "Die before you die"—in other words, come to attention before you are brought to attention. In *Sahih Muslim*, another wording is to consider yourselves already dead; the meaning is the same: wake up now. And this is what mindfulness is really about: coming to attention. One of the words for attention in Arabic is to be awake and aware—you are attentive, your mind is present.

### The clock, eternity, and remembering death — [3:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jSxFQSvFdwU&t=231)

So already by the 1880s, Nietzsche noted: one thinks with one's watch in one's hand. He complained that even as one eats one's midday meal while reading the latest news of the stock market, this is distraction. Everything is done with the clock. One of the most interesting things about time—and one of my favorite writers, Lewis Mumford—said that when the modern mindset came to dominate. This is what he wrote: "Eternity ceased gradually to serve as the measure and focus of human actions. In its place came the dictatorship of the clock." Is that not interesting? People used to think about eternity; their lives were actually determined by their understanding of eternity. And one of the most common things about premodern peoples is that they mention death a lot. And one of the most notable qualities of modern people and postmodern people is that they never talk about death. Why did premodern people talk about death?

When I was in the throne room of the former Queen Elizabeth—now the throne room of King Charles—I was taken there by a Baroness. She is a Muslim in the House of Lords. So they took me into the throne room. I was getting a tour of Parliament, and they have this throne room, and there was this massive clock given by the French king to the British monarch that had the Grim Reaper on top of it. That is what the clock had: the Grim Reaper. In other words, the angel of death was on top of it with his scythe that takes the souls. And if you look—and Dr. Yusuf knows this because he has been working with sundials—in all of the premodern sundials, it will say things like *carpe diem* and many Latin phrases, things like "in one of these hours you will be seized." People saw time as a reminder of their finitude, as the reminder that they were finite beings in temporality. Modern people think they are going to live forever. They do not think about death. And one of the main reasons for this desire for distractibility is that it preoccupies people from thinking about what is inevitable: that they will die.

### The destroyer of delights, the Qur'an, and choosing your attention — [6:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jSxFQSvFdwU&t=407)

The Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) said, "Do much remembrance of the destroyer of delights: death." Not in a morbid sense. This is being-unto-death. This is embracing our mortality, being aware of it. Every night he did death meditation. He would recite poems about death—*al-Habib* and his *diwan*—"be prepared for death and its coming." And they used to sing this on a regular basis to think about death.

The Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) was completely aware of the presence of death. Death permeates the Qur'an. There is not a page that does not have the perfume of death scented on it in the Qur'an. And this is not morbidity. It is not some kind of morbidness. It is actually to make you aware of the preciousness of the time that you have been given. This is the preciousness of life itself. It is a great gift from Allah, exalted is He. And we waste it away with all of this insignificant concern.

One of the interesting things about the ancient Greeks is that they were actually very concerned about distraction. But what they saw was that it was an inner failure to use one's time on what one claimed to value the most. Their reason for treating distraction so seriously was straightforward, and it is the reason we ought to do so too. What you pay attention to will define you. What you pay attention to will define you. Seneca wrote a famous short book called *The Shortness of Life*. He was a great Roman philosopher and one of the notables from Rome.

And he talks about his fellow Romans pursuing political careers. They did not really care about holding elaborate banquets. They did not really enjoy baking their bodies in the sun—I mean, it literally says that; sunbathing is something Europeans tend to do; other peoples do not need to. And to be honest, I suspect he probably was right. But the crucial point is not that it is wrong to choose to spend your time relaxing, whether at the beach or on BuzzFeed. It is that the distracted person is not really choosing at all. That is the key. You are not choosing. It is quite the opposite.

The word in Arabic *ikhtiyar* is one of my favorite Arabic words. It literally means—if you look at it, it comes from *khayr*; *ikhtiyar* is a form in Arabic which is to internalize something. So you are choosing the good. That is what choice is. But it is either a real or an apparent good. That is the difference. And so distracted people are not choosing. They are not choosing a real or even an apparent good. They are surfing; everything will pull their attention. It is called the noonday devil in the old scholastic tradition—sloth, spiritual laziness—people who do not want to think about the inevitable.

### The *Risala*, the *Ihya*, and time as experience — [10:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jSxFQSvFdwU&t=657)

One of the amazing things—and this is what I wanted to really talk about—is one of our most important books, and it really did have an immense impact on Imam al-Ghazali: a book known as the *Risala* of Imam al-Qushayri. This was a foundational book in *tasawwuf*, and *tasawwuf* is part and parcel of the religion of Islam. You cannot take *tasawwuf* out of the religion of Islam without uprooting it. There are many different types of Sufis. There are many Sufis who went astray, just as there are jurists who went astray, and theologians who went astray. Every science has its problems. But the idea somehow that *tasawwuf* like that mentioned in the *Ihya* of Imam al-Ghazali was not from Islam—nobody would have accepted that, including Ibn Taymiyya and Ibn al-Qayyim.

So in the *Ihya* he has a chapter called "Time," and he says that time, for the people who have had realization—these are the verifiers, the people who have penetrated the reality of this religion and have confirmed that it is true, and that if you practice it, you will get the results—in other words, it is falsifiable. This is Popper's argument for a true science: that it can be replicated. So the *muhaqqiqun* are the people who replicate again and again the truths that we are told in our tradition. They verify the religion. They show you that over and over again we do these things and we get the same results. Therefore it is a science. It is not *wahm*. It is not an illusion.

So he says that with the people of *mukashafa*—it is something that is not something tangible; it is something like illusory time, but it is associated with something that is actually substantial. What he means by that is that for us, we do not know if we are going to have the next minute. So I can make my plans for tomorrow. Tomorrow will come for a lot of people, but it is not going to come for everybody. That is the quality that it has. And this is why he was asked: when will the human being finally be still? And he says: he will never be still until he knows that the only time he has is the moment that he is in.

And this is why Allah says about the *siddiqin*—in other words, they have no fear of the future, and they do not grieve. They have no grief about the past because they are here. They are present. They are mindful. They are fully aware. This is the difference between them and everybody else: they are present. So that is what he is telling us.

So he says: a man will say, "I will come at the beginning of next month." He does not really know that. He only hopes he will see it. But the beginning of that next month will come—it will come—but you might not come with it. And that is the nature of time. And then he says: "I heard one of the great *'ulama'* say"—that is exactly what I was saying earlier. What you give your attention to is your reality. This is exactly what he said: the time, the moment—because *waqt* can be "moment"; it can be "time"—this is the Arabic language. Time is what you are in. If you are in the *dunya*, your time is the *dunya*. If you are in the afterlife, your time is the afterlife. If you are in joy, your time is joy. And if you are in grief, your time is grief.

And then he says: he means by this that time is essentially what overwhelms the human being. It is your experience. That is what time is: human experience. And this is what the clock has taken away from us. The clock is this quantifiable thing. Time is not quantifiable, in reality. "Allah is time"—Allah is the eternal now. The scholastics—and I think they took it from us—the scholastics called it *nunc stans*: God is the eternal now. If you look at Ibn 'Arabi, he says: it is the eternal now. It is the now that stands still. "God is time." "Do not curse time, for I am time." Some interpret it as *zaman*. To the outward, *zaman* is what time is to the inward. Anyway, he goes on. It is a stunning thing.

He talks about how Imam al-Shafi'i said: "I learned two things from the Sufis. Time is a sword. If you do not cut with it"—in other words, if you are not attentive—"it will cut you down." We do not kill time. Time kills us. Whenever you hear somebody say, "I am going to go kill some time," that is a person in complete *ghafla*. Whenever you hear somebody—he said—the fool gets up in the morning and says, "What am I going to do today?" And the wise man gets up and says, "What is God going to do with me today?"

So he said: your ego is such that if you do not preoccupy it, focus it on the good, it will focus you on the harmful, on the evil, on the bad. Allah—again—said: "Time is a file. It just wears you down. It does not obliterate you. It wears you down till there is nothing left." And this is why time is so important. He ends this by saying: every day that passes takes a part of me, and it gives remorse to my heart—in other words, for the time I wasted—and then it continues on. It continues on.

And then he says: it is like the people of the Fire whose skin is reborn. And this is from the Qur'an: every time the skin gets burnt, Allah recreates it. We know about these proprioceptors—all the skin has these receptors. That is why people who get burnt really deeply do not feel it once they have gone through the skin. So the skin, according to the Qur'an, is recreated so that the receptors experience it again. And then he says: the dead one is not the one who dies and then finds repose. Rather, the dead are the dead among the living. The intelligent one is the one who is mindful of his time. He is giving time its due. So he is either in *salah* or in *dhikr*, depending on what time it is.

These are very high meanings and insights that should be looked at according to one's station. So what is asked of people who are content with being common? We should all aspire to rise higher than our position. And "common people" here is not the street sweeper—the street sweeper might be a knower of God. "Common people" in the Western understanding are people who do not have degrees, have not gone to college, have not gotten PhDs—very often those are the most common people in the spiritual sense. They are the most *'awamm*.
