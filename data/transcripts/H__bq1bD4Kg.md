## The school of eternity: tribulation, patience, and the believer

### Sacred text messages and “read the eternities” — [0:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H__bq1bD4Kg&t=17)

Part of what I have called the “sacred text messages”: we are living in a time of text messages, and they seem to be taking people away from the text messages we received from God. Everybody is reading their text messages from created things, and they have forgotten the text messages from the Creator of things. That is one of the great calamities.

Henry David Thoreau said: do not read the *Times*—read the eternities. People are obsessed with news, with the latest thing—“Did you hear?” “Did you know?” “So-and-so is doing that”—“Can you believe that?” “Did you hear Cardi B’s latest…?” It is a kind of madness. We are trying to get back to the real text messages.

So today I want to look at a couple of text messages from God and His Messenger (peace and blessings upon him). They belong to the same topic.

### The Qur’an on tribulation, patience, and gratitude — [2:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H__bq1bD4Kg&t=125)

The first is from the Qur’an. Allah, exalted is He, speaks in *Surat al-Baqarah*—actually a passage of verses, 155 to 157. Commentators say this is really an announcement; the *mufassirun* call it an *indhar*, a forewarning. Sometimes bells go off; you have tsunami warnings—in Indonesia they have alarms. This is like God’s alarm to let us know that tribulation is coming. The Companions understood that. Tribulation is part of life; it is at the heart of life, because the Qur’an tells us so.

Even the prophets knew fear. When the angels came to Ibrahim, peace be upon him, the Qur’an says he felt fear in his heart. Prophets feel fear, but they are courageous, because courage is not the absence of fear—it is that you do not let fear incapacitate you. The firefighter who rushes in does fear the fire; courage overrides that human fear.

We will be tested with fear—fear of losing a job when the economy tanks, fear for a loved one who is sick—natural in the human condition. Then Allah mentions hunger. Hunger was far more present in the pre-modern world. We live in an age of cornucopia for many; there are still places that should not lack, given what we know—but civilizations have rarely had such sustenance paired with such ingratitude. Traditionally people had deep gratitude. In Muslim cultures people often stressed finishing one’s plate and leaving nothing wasted, out of esteem for blessing, because the Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) had such regard for Allah’s blessings. Once he saw bread on the ground and told ‘A’ishah: be a good neighbor to the blessings of Allah—stay aware of constant blessing.

Then the verse speaks of *nuqsan*—loss, diminishment. *Nuqs* contrasts with *ziyadah* (increase): we will be tested with loss of wealth, loss of life. Some read “loss of life” only as death, but it can mean diminishment of life—you can lose a tooth, a hand, a leg; you can lose those you love or your own life. Then *thamarat*—the fruits of life—we will be tested with them.

Then: give glad tidings to those who are patient in tribulation. The response to blessing is gratitude; the response to tribulation is patience. Many Muslims say, “How long must we be patient?” In our tradition patience means patience in obedience and patience in not falling into disobedience—holding yourself back.

### Kierkegaard, *ibtila’*, and turning to Allah — [6:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H__bq1bD4Kg&t=394)

Kierkegaard wrote an essay he called *The School of Eternity*. He begins by quoting Hebrews—that the Prophet learned obedience from what he suffered (Hebrews 5:8). Kierkegaard says: if obedience followed immediately on suffering, the lesson would be easy; but learning obedience is not that easy. Humanly speaking, suffering is dangerous—but more terrible is failing to learn obedience. Suffering is dangerous schooling only if you do not learn obedience. Then it is terrible, like the strongest medicine producing the wrong reaction; in that danger a person needs God’s help. Otherwise he may learn what is most corrupting: craven despondency, deadening of noble fervor, defiance, despair.

When tribulations come we are meant to learn obedience—to become more obedient. Kierkegaard calls that the school of eternity: when there is suffering and obedience within suffering, you are being educated for eternity. With patience in obedience and patience in avoiding sinfulness, there is no restless hankering in the soul, no restlessness of sin or of sorrow.

In our tradition we would say *ibtila’* (trial) is like a guardian angel keeping you from slipping into the fragmentariness of the world—the fragmentariness that tears the soul apart. Suffering keeps you in this dangerous school so you may be educated for eternity. Patience in tribulation and gratitude in blessing align you vertically with God; you are ready to meet your Lord. If you only complain—“Why me?”—the ancients would still have had fear and trepidation, but not that tone.

In Mauritania, people sometimes assumed that as a Westerner I might know medicine—one reason I later studied to return and help. They would describe their illness and then say, “I am only telling you—I am not complaining.” They wanted help but drew a line: not complaining. That reflects knowing we belong to God; body and soul are in His dominion (*mulk*), and He does with His dominion as He wills. We should ask Allah for blessings and fear ingratitude. The whining today is striking—especially where there is so much opportunity, yet so much complaint about conditions. Are you in a state of obedience? If you truly suffer, the lesson is to turn to Allah.

The pagan Romans suspended the Senate during thunderstorms out of fear of divine wrath. Our Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) would pace in *dhikr* of Allah in storms, knowing awe and punishment can accompany such signs. Pagans went to temples to expiate and ask forgiveness—yet we have been given clearer guidance.

In the verses: when calamity strikes, the patient say we belong to Allah and to Him we return. No calamity afflicts you but by Allah’s decree; another verse speaks of what arises from yourselves—they are not mutually exclusive; adab with God includes blaming the self while recognizing the decree. That phrase reminds you that you will return to Allah. They receive Allah’s blessings and mercy; they are the rightly guided.

### The hadith of the believer, privilege as trial, and Ahmad al-Zarruq — [14:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H__bq1bD4Kg&t=858)

Then comes the remarkable hadith. Allah calls them rightly guided. The hadith is in *Sahih Muslim*. Imam Muslim—among the six canonical compilers, a student of Imam al-Bukhari, second in many rankings—used slightly less stringent conditions than Bukhari: for Muslim, if narrators were in the same city at the same time, that could suffice, whereas Bukhari wanted proof they actually met. If they were upright, Muslim could accept that they were contemporaries in the same city.

The narration comes through Suhaib—called “the Byzantine” or “the Roman”; some argue an Arab lineage, but what strikes me is that the Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) had Companions from Africa, from Rome, from Persia—representing major civilizations; the Indo-European world spans India through Persia and Rome.

The Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) said: how wondrous is the affair of the believer—all his affair is good, and that is for none but the believer. When good reaches him he is grateful, and that is good for him; when harm reaches him—calamity, disease, loss of wealth, what the verses mention—he shows patience, and that is better for him. So the hadith teaches: for the believer it is all good; even harm is good because there is *hikmah* behind it. You hear “it’s all good” from people in very hard situations—that deep resilience, a kind of wisdom the hadith names.

Christopher Reeve, who played Superman, had a terrible accident, broke his neck, was fully paralyzed. Asked how he coped, he said you can see the world as meaningful or meaningless; he chose meaningful, and became an advocate for disabled people. He turned tribulation into service—I do not know how much that drew on his Christian background, but a believer does something like that. We would not wish such trials on anyone. I met a remarkable young Iraqi man in Medina, paralyzed by a bomb—he lives in the United States with his father; his spirit was beautiful, his acceptance profound. Bombing civilians is wrong; I personally believe aerial bombardment should be judged a war crime under international law, though it is not yet—regardless, he was a victim of wrong, and as a believer he lived the hadith: how wondrous is the affair of the believer.

Life on earth is short. We forget: tribulation is not only poverty and pain—wealth and privilege are trials. The Qur’an says Allah has favored some over others; how is privilege used? “We made some of you a trial for others—will you show patience?” Some say the poor are a trial for the rich and the rich for the poor, the ignorant for the learned and the learned for the ignorant—we could go on. Will we live in accord with God and His Messenger, or only react to the world with heat?

Finally, something that struck me from the first time I read it: Ahmad al-Zarruq, one of my favorite scholars, in his *Qawa’id*—the sixty-fifth principle. Allah’s uniqueness in perfection entails imperfection in everything else. No creature is perfect unless He perfects him; the Prophet’s perfection is grace from Allah, not an inherent property. Imperfection is the norm; perfection is exceptional. Do not expect your spouse or children to be perfect—to demand that is wrong.

Seeking perfection as the norm of this world is false. People who want a perfect world are really asking for *Jannah*; that is not *dunya*. *Dunya* is the lowest abode—we are in the place where you empty foulness, like the place you rid yourself of waste. It is all upward from here; even in Hell people remember Allah, in *dhikr* and *nadama* (regret)—a painful but lucid state. Accept *dunya* as it is.

It is said: look at people as if they were perfect, and treat imperfection as their nature; if perfection appears, it is *fadl*—grace. Some excel in *adab*, honesty, character in patches, and fall short elsewhere—that is normal.

### Imam al-Junayd’s axiom, Paradise and Hell, and the eye of the hurricane — [23:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H__bq1bD4Kg&t=1418)

Through that outlook, prudence, good feeling, companionship, and overlooking faults become possible—you keep friends when they fall short. David Niven said of Errol Flynn: you could always count on him to let you down. Some people are like that; knowing it is useful—that is *dunya*; show compassion.

Imam al-Junayd said: I took as an axiom—I no longer find repulsive whatever comes to me from the world. This abode is the abode of distress, tribulation, strife, *fitna*—trials, civil strife. The abode is not “evil” in a crude sense; it is deficient—the Arabs called poverty *shidda* because of lack. It is an abode of want. Judgment upon the world is that it keeps bringing what the ego dislikes.

One of the most extraordinary hadiths: Paradise is surrounded by what is repulsive to the self; Hell is surrounded by desires. Part of our work here is to refuse what pleases now but harms later—like cake for a diabetic: sweet now, harmful over time. Much of what tastes good is bad for you; what is good for you is often less immediately pleasant—a metaphor for sin and obedience. Sugar rots teeth and harms health yet everyone loves it; appetites drag people toward Hell when they chase wants over needs.

When the world greets you with what you love, that is grace. The basis of *dunya* is tribulation—like the first noble truth in Buddhism; the second, that attachment fuels suffering. Kierkegaard said we must be weaned from the world—the weaning child cries, then grows. Death weans us by force. Our tradition says: consider yourselves already dead—wean yourself willingly. Accept the world. Someone said disappointment equals reality plus expectation; expect little from the world and you suffer less—or expect the worst. The New Age “Secret” style positive thinking is not the whole story; the Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) was profoundly positive—when asked about evil he said, ask me about good—yet he knew the nature of the abode. Tribulations did not scatter him. That steadiness is the eye of the hurricane: God made the storm and the still center; *dunya* is the storm; acceptance is the center.

One of my father’s teachers was asked: can a man be happy knowing the world is full of suffering? He said yes—the happy man knows the world is suffering and is happy despite it. How? So much misery comes from unhappy people; do not add to it. There is a moral obligation to be cheerful. The Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) loved the name Sa‘id (“happy”); hearing it would lighten him. Do not be among the *ashqiya’* (wretched/miserable); be among the *su‘ada’* (felicitous). Rejoice in the guidance we have been given.

We have a pillar that cannot be destroyed: the Messenger of Allah. Cling to the rope Allah gave—the Qur’an commands it. Some text messages, God willing, and reflections on them. May Allah bless you. The intro and outro are from my late friend Hamza al-Awwad, a beautiful Nubian brother who brought many to Islam through his singing—may Allah have mercy on him.
