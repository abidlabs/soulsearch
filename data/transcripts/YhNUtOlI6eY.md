## Don’t be triggered: the Qur’an, feelings, and self-mastery

### Al-Baqarah 2:216 — what you dislike may be good for you — [0:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YhNUtOlI6eY&t=16)

Another sacred text message. The verse I want to look at is in *Surat al-Baqarah*, the 216th verse: *Kutiba ‘alaykum al-qital*—fighting, which in our tradition includes defending the faith, is prescribed for you; it is incumbent. Yet it is hateful to you. *Wa `asa an takrahū shay’an wa huwa khayrun lakum*—perhaps you dislike a thing while it is good for you; *wa `asa an tuhibbū shay’an wa huwa sharrun lakum*—perhaps you love a thing while it is harmful for you. *Wa Allāhu ya‘lamu wa antum lā ta‘lamūn*—Allah knows and you do not know.

That connects to something traditional psychology recognized—and I learned from a brilliant sinologist and scholar, Dr. Thomas Cleary—the interplay of thinking and feeling. Two capacities constantly interact, from outside and inside. People today say “triggered”: we are affected by others, by what they say.

### Triggering, the *hakim*’s lesson, and two paths of reprocessing — [1:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YhNUtOlI6eY&t=113)

There is a story of a Pakistani *hakim* who treated with *ruqyah*. A physician treated a patient who then recovered. The doctor said he was glad the medicine worked; she said the medicine did not help her. What helped? “The *hakim* did *ruqyah* for me—that cured me.” The doctor, a materialist, wanted to meet the *hakim* and tell him he was misleading people with “mumbo jumbo”—we have science now.

The *hakim* listened to the lecture about magical words and superstition, then said, “You’re a donkey.” The doctor flushed, puffed up—“How dare you—I trained at King Edward Medical College,” and so on. The *hakim* pressed: worse than a donkey. The doctor was about to explode. The *hakim* said: look—only words changed your whole inner state; I gave you nothing physical. How can words do that?

That is about triggering, thinking, and feeling. People with bad intentions can exploit how suggestible we are—excitement, arrested attention—a kind of possession. Individuals, groups, even nations can fall under manipulation when unconscious vulnerability is high.

There are two classic models of emotional reprocessing; they show how intellectual and emotional development intertwine. One traditional path is to rethink likes and dislikes—exactly what the verse commands.

Consider “emotional logic.” Someone wrongs you; you feel sorrow, then self-pity—you are the victim; that fuels anger; then blame; then rationalization—“I am justified.” Feelings hijack the inner life; you lose control—a kind of mind control. Denise Winn’s *The Manipulated Mind* is worth reading; rulers use such techniques. There is even a Chinese manual for emperors with a chapter on letting “all hell break loose”—sometimes states allow a purge so pent-up frustration vents, like lancing a boil.

The other model is “logical emotion”: you detach, step back—“What is happening inside me? Why do I feel ‘triggered’?”—reflect, and that can open compassion. That is a free person’s response. The first is the default.

### Default settings, the Qur’an as manual, and mass manipulation — [6:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YhNUtOlI6eY&t=380)

We arrive with default settings—like new devices. One default is “me, me, me”—children scream when they do not get what they want. Good teachers reset the defaults. The operating manual for the human being is the Qur’an—you learn yourself and reprogram. “When they are angry they forgive”—you do not have to stay on one channel of blame. Someone watching only one TV channel their whole life does not know others exist until someone changes the channel—“What do you mean, channel?”—like being stuck on empty programming while nature documentaries, Mecca, and Qur’an recitation were available all along. Getting off the default is life’s challenge. Anger-and-blame cycles rooted in self-pity make you easy to divide and manipulate.

Mixing likes and dislikes with judgments of good and evil is a vulnerability. The Qur’an trains you: consider what is bad in what you love and good in what you dislike. Dr. Cleary notes that when that exercise becomes an ordinary reflex, it is harder to be fooled by emotional appeals dressed as reason.

A psychiatrist from Ghana—traditional family, father a scholar, himself an MD in psychiatry—once heard a Muslim speaker shouting in agitation at a conference and said: how tragic to use a public platform to vent personal pathology—not backbiting; he pitied the man. Much public noise comes from unwell people; do not let yourself be psychologically manipulated.

Individuals and groups fixate on outside triggers; we live in an age of manipulation. Let us not forget: multitudes follow the Antichrist—*atbā‘*. The Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) spoke of what the Antichrist would bring; narrations use wording that in translation has been rendered in ways people now compare to “drones”—something worth sober reflection.

### Servants of the Merciful, inner freedom, and “read the eternities” — [15:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YhNUtOlI6eY&t=936)

The idea of not being “triggered” is in the Qur’an. Allah says the servants of the Merciful walk gently on the earth; when ignorant people address them, they say, “Peace.” When they hear vain talk, they pass by with dignity—commentators say they overlook it. The Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) said the best people mix with others and endure their harm—that is the world; people will bother you. Do not let them own your inner state.

Allah says: We made some of you a trial for others—will you show patience? Patience moves you from the default of impatience, anger, blame, and victimhood to what Allah gave in the “operating manual”—reprogramming to a higher setting. Do not say “you’re triggering me.” Who are they to control your inner world?

Kierkegaard said people demand freedom of speech as compensation for lacking freedom of thought. Your inner world can be yours if you learn to govern it; otherwise others will. Take account of yourself; the Prophet (peace and blessings upon him) told us to govern the tongue. The Qur’an tells us to save ourselves from ourselves—do not let the elephant ride you; you ride the elephant, or it will crush you.

Reflection matters—why am I upset? I once read with Murabit al-Hajj the line about struggling with the self for the Lord of the worlds and asked: what if the self overwhelms you? He said: you must struggle—that is the point. Allah does not command what is beyond capacity. Keep struggling until you gain mastery. Do not let others manipulate you—especially when mass media and narratives are so unreliable.

The best advice I know from a Western voice for this is Thoreau’s about newspapers: do not read the *Times*—read the eternities. Today everyone reads the *Times* on phones and screens—the *New York Times*, the *London Times*, the internet. We have a book of eternities—the Qur’an. It is difficult; nothing worth having comes easy. The easy things are defaults: overeat, overspeak, waste time, be lazy. Changing defaults is struggle. “Those who struggle in Our path—we guide them to Our ways”—paths of righteousness. May Allah make us people of righteousness.
